Search Help

The search system for "Darwin Online" consists of two parts. The first part, "free text search", which searches for words appearing in the digital text of the items on the site, is very similar to that operated by general Web search engines such as Google or Yahoo.

(Free text search within the body of the texts)

Into this input box on the Search pages, or in the "Search" box at the head of each page on the website, you may type a set of words you wish to locate within the digital texts. By default these will be found as isolated words - if you wish to find a connected phrase, surround the phrase in inverted commas, like so - "origin of species".

The second part of the search system, which appears only on the search pages themselves, relates the bibliographic records and metadata held about the items on the site to the digital texts themselves.


Date in format YYYY-MM-DD - e.g. 1859-11-24 (MM, DD optional)

For example, entering a date into this box will restrict the returned items to those dated after the entered date (considered inclusively). You may type either simply a four-digit year, a year followed by a month, or a precise date.

In combination with the "Before date:" field, for example, you may restrict the returned results to only those from a single year, say 1859, by typing the same year into both "After date:" and "Before date:".

The other fields and controls on the search pages work similarly - in general they are all "restrictive" in that they will narrow down the returned set of results, for example to those in a particular language or of a particular type.

Note that although you may use the free text, and field-based search controls alternatively, or simultaneously, whether any text is typed into the "free text" field determines the fundamental nature of the search, that is, the type of hit that will be returned. If you enter free text keywords, the hits returned will be to specific pages in the digital text, whereas if you do not, the hits will consist of bibliographic records representing items as a whole, from which further links will lead you to the digital text and facsimile if these are stored on the site.

Note that there are currently many more items (around 34,000) held in the bibliographic databases than those for which digital text or images are held (currently around 500).
